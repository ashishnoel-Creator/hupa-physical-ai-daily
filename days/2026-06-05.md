### Data collection

- **The collection-side signal today is the human-video survey, and it reads the way the spring ego-data cluster has read all along.** The survey's whole premise is that robot demonstrations do not scale because they are expensive and tied to one embodiment, and that human video is the substrate that does scale, from YouTube instructional sets to lightweight wearable capture. The hard part it identifies is not collecting the video, it is recovering usable action information from it. Latent actions, world models, 2D supervision and 3D reconstruction are all just different ways to pull control signal out of messy human footage. The capture-side read is direct. Footage that recovers hands, contacts and 3D structure cleanly is worth multiples of footage that does not, and that gap is set at capture time against a spec, not in post. This is the argument for hardware-led validated capture, not undifferentiated hours. [arXiv 2606.00054](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.00054)

### Research papers worth a look

- **From Human Videos to Robot Manipulation: A Survey on Scalable Vision-Language-Action Learning with Human-Centric Data (arXiv 2606.00054), Zhiyuan Feng et al.** A unified map of how the field turns human video into VLA training signal, sorted by the action information each method derives: latent action representations, predictive world models, explicit 2D supervision, and explicit 3D reconstruction. The thesis to carry is that robot demonstrations are costly and embodiment-locked while human video is abundant and rich, so the bottleneck is the transformation step, not the supply. Read for the taxonomy, because it tells you exactly which capture qualities (clean hands, contacts, recoverable 3D) make footage usable for each method, and that is the spec a capture business should be selling against. [arXiv 2606.00054](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.00054)
- **Face versus Body Tracking for Human-Robot Interaction: An Egocentric Dataset (arXiv 2606.03694), RO-MAN 2026.** A smaller egocentric dataset comparing face-tracking against body-tracking for the attention problem in human-robot interaction. Narrow, but it lands in the same week as the human-video survey and it is another datapoint that the field treats egocentric capture as the default lens for interaction data. Worth a glance for anyone building capture rigs that have to decide what the camera should be locked onto. [arXiv 2606.03694](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.03694)
