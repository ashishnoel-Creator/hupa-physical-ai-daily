    Data collection

    • X Square Robot is the capture-and-pipeline signal of the day, and the number to hold is the 95% data-cost cut. The company has stood up its own data-collection factory with an end-to-end pipeline that automates large-scale gathering, cleaning, labelling and quality control, all feeding its WALL-B foundation model. The hardware doing the capture is its proprietary XR Zero G0 device, which it credits with reducing the cost of training data by 95%. Read against the thesis, this is the same move the China column keeps surfacing: the leading embodied firms are vertically integrating the data layer rather than buying it, and they are pairing capture hardware with an in-house validation loop. The XR Zero G0 device itself was logged earlier (it is already in the Papers DB), but the part that is new is the factory wrapped around it and the cost claim attached. A 95% reduction, if it holds outside a deck, is a direct statement that whoever owns the capture device owns the unit economics of the data. Gasgoo

The pitch is explicit that vision datasets teach a robot to see but not to handle contact, and that the missing layer is data on what to do at the moment of touch. ViTai is building the sensor, the actuator and the data-acquisition-plus-model closed loop together, and one of its three named customer groups is research and model teams buying its gear specifically as data-collection equipment. A capture layer for force and contact, sold as instrumentation, is adjacent to where an egocentric capture thesis sits. Gasgoo

    Embedded AI systems

    • The Gasgoo chip piece is the embedded read of the day, and its core claim is that the whole industry is measuring the wrong thing. The dominant narrative is the compute race: Jetson Thor at 2,070 FP4 TFLOPS, Qualcomm’s Yuelong IQ10 at 700 TOPS, Black Sesame’s A2000X at an equivalent 1,000 TOPS. The argument is that cloud inference chases throughput while a robot executing a precise grasp needs latency determinism, a stable and predictable response time per command, which does not show up in a TOPS figure at all. Take tactile feedback: the force signal has to round-trip from sensor to joint motor in milliseconds or the robot drops or crushes the object, and that interrupt-latency budget is invisible in the headline compute number. This is splitting the hardware into a “big brain” (perception, planning, multimodal, heavy compute, relaxed timing) and a “cerebellum” (motion control and real-time feedback, light compute, extreme latency and power demands). On the brain side NVIDIA’s Halos for Robotics now bundles chips, OS and safety certification with 43 companies signed on. On the cerebellum side the domestic firms have an opening, because those chips sit closer to MCUs on mature nodes: CAS Wireless Semiconductor’s analog-architecture cerebellum ASIC claims to drop key control-command latency from 2.8ns to 0.2ns, and its April CT-21X is billed as China’s first dedicated GaN magnetic-encoding chip for humanoid limb control. The read for us is that the embedded stack is bifurcating, and the layer where capture and contact data actually matter is the real-time one nobody can fake with raw TOPS. Gasgoo
    • The in-house-chip move got two concrete data points in June, both about OEMs trying to own the silicon: UBTECH and Maxxiri formed a JV, Xixuan Chuangzhi, targeting tape-out in H2 2027 and mass production in 2028, and Li Auto shipped its self-developed Mahe M100 while calling the car an “embodied AI terminal.” UBTECH’s stated driver is cost: chairman Zhou Jian says foreign chips and materials run about one-third of a humanoid’s BOM, and that general-purpose silicon is not optimized for robots. The counter, from Intel Labs China’s Song Jiqiang, is that the robot market is still too small to make dedicated chips profitable and VLA is too unsettled to lock in silicon. Whether dedicated wins turns on whether VLA and world models converge in the next three to five years. For a capture layer the relevant part is that the BOM pressure is pushing the whole stack toward domestic, vertically-integrated hardware, which is the same direction the data layer is moving. Gasgoo

    Robotics & humanoids

    • X Square Robot is the deployment-plus-capital signal, and the deployment half is homes: its WALL-AS and WALL-B robots are working alongside cleaners in real households via a partnership with 58.com. WALL-B is a unified world-model architecture that folds vision, language, motion and physical prediction into a single network trained from scratch, and the company frames the household push as the first large-scale embodied deployment in a consumer-facing real-world setting. It has also formed a JV with Holike (Guangdong Zhimilianbian Robot Co.) to ride Holike’s ~100,000 annual home-renovation users into homes.
