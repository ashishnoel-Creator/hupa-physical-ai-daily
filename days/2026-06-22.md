### TL;DR

- **The brief has been wrong about one thing for weeks, and this run corrects it: there already is a funded Indian entrant running hardware-led ego capture at scale, and it is the closest thing to a direct competitor this feed has seen.** Human Archive, a YC W26 startup founded by four Berkeley and Stanford dropouts, raised $8.2M (Wing VC, NVP Capital, YC, plus angels from OpenAI, Nvidia, Google, Mercor, BAIR and SAIL) and is running 1,000+ head-mounted cameras and wearable sensors on Indian gig workers across homes, hotels, restaurants, retail, construction and industrial sites. ### Data collection

- **Human Archive is the catch-up that matters, because it is the local capture entrant this brief kept saying did not exist.** The model the thesis runs on is that India's hands, homes and environments are missing from every public ego corpus, and that no funded local player was building to close that gap. That second half is now false. Human Archive has been running over a thousand ego rigs on Indian gig workers since before this feed started, collecting first-person video and sensor data across the exact messy environments the thesis names, then shipping it to frontier labs. CEO Raj Patel says the company has already delivered datasets to research teams. The capture-first framing is identical to ours, down to the split between raw video and aligned multimodal signal. [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/26/human-archive-taps-into-indias-services-startups-to-collect-data-for-physical-ai/), [Inc42](https://inc42.com/startups/inside-human-archive-the-startup-recording-indian-workers-to-train-the-worlds-robots/)
- **The HA-Multi spec is worth reading line by line against our own rig.** Human Archive describes HA-Multi as a fully aligned multimodal dataset with vision, stereo depth via IR dot projection, tactile gloves, body IMUs and wrist cameras, and calls it the largest multimodal dataset of its kind. That is a richer sensor bundle than RGB-plus-IMU, and it is the same direction the geometry-native argument (GAM, June 19) pointed at: depth and contact captured at source, not inferred from flat video. The honest read for us is that the bar on what a serious ego dataset includes has already moved past video, and a competitor is shipping the richer bundle today. [YC launch](https://www.ycombinator.com/launches/PeP-human-archive-the-world-s-largest-multimodal-robotics-dataset)
- **The labor economics are the part to sit with.** Human Archive reportedly pays workers about $1 per hour, against $2.63 to $4.20 at rival firms, and Patel claimed that when a smaller services platform offered subsidised bookings in exchange for consent-based recording, nearly 98% of users opted in. Cheap consent at scale is the thing that makes the Indian capture pipeline economically real and ethically fraught at the same time. Whoever runs this layer in India is going to be priced against a dollar an hour and judged against that consent question. [Inc42](https://inc42.com/startups/inside-human-archive-the-startup-recording-indian-workers-to-train-the-worlds-robots/), [Entrackr](https://entrackr.com/in-depth/snabbit-confirms-exploring-physical-ai-proposal-with-human-archive-denies-home-rollout-11870375)

### Data annotation & validation

- **The validation story inside Human Archive is consent and provenance, not labeling.** Human Archive says its contracts comply with the DPDP Act, that it displays privacy notices and consent information, and that faces are blurred in recordings. That is a validation function in the legal sense: the auditable trail that the data was collected with informed consent is becoming as load-bearing as the QC that the footage meets spec. For an audit-trailed capture business this is the same argument the thesis makes about technical QC, extended to provenance. A buyer in 2026 wants both the clip and the proof it was lawfully obtained. [CoinReporter](https://www.coinreporter.io/2026/05/human-archive-lands-8-2m-for-robot-training-data-amid-india-privacy-probe/)

### India & SEA spotlight

- **Human Archive resets the India picture, so the spotlight leads with it even though the news is weeks old.** The structural claim this brief has repeated, that Indian robotics capital flows to bodies while the capture layer underneath has no local entrant, is half wrong. The bodies half still holds (General Autonomy, Addverb). The capture half does not. There is a funded, operating, frontier-lab-backed Indian ego-capture company, and it is moving fast and cheap. For HuPa that changes the competitive map more than any humanoid demo would. The differentiator is no longer "first local capture player," it is operating discipline, the consent and audit trail, and which environments and demographics get covered cleanly. [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/26/human-archive-taps-into-indias-services-startups-to-collect-data-for-physical-ai/), [Inc42](https://inc42.com/startups/inside-human-archive-the-startup-recording-indian-workers-to-train-the-worlds-robots/)
- **Addverb is the fresh India money datapoint, and the tell is that a body-maker is naming data collection as a use of funds.** Reliance-backed Addverb is planning a $100M+ raise, its first since 2021, to build humanoid and quadruped robots, launch its own lidar, collect data and build AI to train the machines. Revenue is tracking to about $136M this fiscal year on a ~$200M order book. A serious Indian robotics company is now vertically reaching down into the data layer rather than treating it as someone else's problem, which is the same convergence the China stack keeps showing. [Business Today](https://www.businesstoday.in/technology/news/story/addverb-plans-100-million-fundraise-as-it-targets-global-robotics-growth-536290-2026-06-11)
