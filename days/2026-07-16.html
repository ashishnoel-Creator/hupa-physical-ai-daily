Changan’s deputy chief engineer: “Define datasets by scenario, then use high-quality datasets to boost model capability.” That is buyers writing our spec sheet for us. Gasgoo

    Data collection

    • The Gasgoo automaker panel is the clearest demand-side statement yet on how factory data loops will actually run, and it cuts against the bulk-collection playbook. Galaxy General’s industrial GM Cang Yu said collection, training and inference should happen primarily at the edge with continuous pipelines for data and model evolution. Changan’s Tan Yingcong said OEMs keep data on-premise and avoid cloud training for security, and framed the industry push as “data consistency” across scenarios rather than volume. FMC³’s co-founder went further: “the last mile of data” must remain in national or corporate hands, and the industry needs standards for industrial data ownership and IP. Every one of those constraints favors capture partners who show up with a spec, an audit trail and edge-side processing over anyone shipping raw footage to a cloud. Gasgoo
    • RoboParty’s freshly funded stack is half a data play. Its recently open-sourced Party OS toolchain (MimicLite, UFO, action retargeting tools) explicitly targets lowering barriers to data collection, simulation training and real-world deployment, and Gasgoo’s read of the CATL investment is that RoboParty’s hardware gets task feedback inside CATL’s manufacturing and energy scenarios as a closed data loop. A component supplier buying its way into the loop where deployment data gets generated is the same pattern the JD and Tashan deals showed, now at China’s most important battery maker. Gasgoo

    Data annotation & validation

    • Validation showed up on the buyer side of the Gasgoo panel in two forms worth logging. Changan is pushing “data consistency,” meaning accuracy of scenario data across environments before it is used to train, which is a QC function whoever supplies the data will have to perform. And Galaxy General’s Cang Yu set the bar for robot manufacturing itself: robot production still needs in-process checks, and “when robot production lines move straight to final inspection, that’s when scale delivery truly arrives.” Graded, inspection-ready processes are becoming the shared language between robot makers and their buyers. Gasgoo
    • NVIDIA published the most detailed argument yet that robot evaluation is a statistics problem, and the numbers cut against every demo-reel claim in the market. RoboLab, the Seattle research lab’s benchmarking platform behind the open-source Isaac Lab-Arena framework, grades policies with partial credit for subtasks, logs failure events (wrong grasps, drops, collisions) at the frame level, and runs sensitivity analysis to attribute failures to specific scene variables. The statistical core: a 90% success rate measured over 70 rollouts carries a Clopper-Pearson confidence interval 15.4 points wide, and tightening that to 2 points takes roughly 1,030 rollouts, far more than most published benchmarks run. Also on the record: no policy NVIDIA tested completes more than four complex subtasks in sequence, and vague instructions consistently break current models. Graded scoring, failure attribution and statistical confidence are the same disciplines a data vendor gets audited on, now standardized by the biggest vendor in the room. NVIDIA, The Robot Report

    Robotics & humanoids

    • Walden Robotics is the launch of the window, and the design choices matter more than the round size. Tedrake, former SVP of Large Behavior Models at TRI and the researcher behind Diffusion Policy, spun the team out in January, had robots doing production work at a Toyota plant by February (“first pilot to real work in under two months”), and shipped a robot with a wheeled or fixed base and no legs. Strategic partners span automotive, aerospace, semiconductors, electronics, logistics and life sciences. The founding thesis is continuous learning on the job, which means the robot is also the collection rig and the deployment contract is also a data contract. BusinessWire, The Next Web, Boston Globe

    • The wheels-first consensus is now explicit on both sides of the Pacific. The same day Walden launched leg-free, FMC³’s co-founder told the Gasgoo panel that the first robots to scale in auto plants over the next three to five years will be wheeled dual-arm forms, not bipeds, and SAIC-GM’s equipment lead has been making the same argument publicly. Galaxy General’s Cang Yu expects a “Design for Robot” re-architecture of factory layouts around 2027, and FAW has stood up its own embodied intelligence subsidiary to manage the transition. Gasgoo

    • Stella-Robot put a 16,999 yuan price tag on a 20-DoF dexterous hand and sold the first 88 units in two days. The Gaia Hand 20 is built from 999-yuan integrated drive-control joint modules (16mm diameter, 0.1ms response, bundles as low as 798 yuan) with five-minute joint-level swaps, weighs 790g and handles 15kg. Its cable-driven Pantheon Hand 20 sibling relocates drives off the palm to hit 290g with 500N grip. Overseas high-end hands cost hundreds of thousands to millions of yuan, and Gasgoo frames the launch as a direct run at that monopoly. The company’s full stack notably includes embodied data acquisition systems alongside the hands. Gasgoo

    • UBTech’s U1 companion humanoid will be sold through Mercedes-Benz 4S dealerships. Boshi Group, a Mercedes strategic partner in China, became UBTech’s first authorized partner for the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei region and will retail and lease the U1 through its Mercedes and Hyundai dealer network plus its “Mechanical Eden” robot experience stores. Pricing runs 119,800 yuan (Lite) to 990,000 yuan (Ultra male model), and total U1 orders passed 13,361 units by end of June, less than a month after pre-sales opened. Luxury car dealerships turning into humanoid showrooms is a distribution channel nobody priced in. Gasgoo

    • PrimeBOT officially launched the Qiyuan T1, the transformable personal robot this column flagged as a WAIC marker on Tuesday, and the technical detail is the interesting part. A single chassis switches autonomously between quadruped (outdoor terrain) and wheel-foot humanoid (indoor) forms with no restart, driven by one Transformer-based cross-morph locomotion base model managing balance and posture across both morphologies. One policy, two bodies is a data-efficiency claim as much as a hardware trick. Pricing undisclosed, public showcase at WAIC. Gasgoo

    • Agility went to Washington with a six-point policy ask, and the subtext is that the US humanoid industry now wants what Korea and China already have. CEO Peggy Johnson’s recommendations: domestic manufacturing support for key components (which US developers currently must source internationally), targeted incentives so small and midsize manufacturers can adopt, government procurement pathways for non-weaponized humanoids in logistics and defense sustainment, supply chain resilience, a coordinated national strategy, and industry-led voluntary safety standards. Two weeks after Seoul put $880B and a 20% humanoid market share target on paper, the leading US humanoid company is asking its own government for a plan. The Robot Report

    Research papers worth a look

    Coverage note: the arXiv cs.RO listing cache still shows Monday July 13 as the newest announcement batch (third run in a row), and Hugging Face’s Daily Papers for July 15 carried no robotics entries, so no fresh batch was reachable. Instead, today back-fills the two papers behind the Walden launch, which the Papers DB did not yet hold:

    • Diffusion Policy: Visuomotor Policy Learning via Action Diffusion (arXiv 2303.04137, RSS 2023; Cheng Chi, Shuran Song, Russ Tedrake et al., Columbia and TRI). The paper that made denoising diffusion the default action head for imitation learning, with a 46.9% average improvement over prior methods across 12 tasks. Three years later it is the acknowledged foundation of a $1.1B company. arXiv
    • A Careful Examination of Large Behavior Models for Multitask Dexterous Manipulation (arXiv 2507.05331, July 2025; TRI LBM Team, Tedrake senior author). The LBM tech report behind Walden’s model class: multi-task pretraining on mixed sim and real data makes policies more robust and teaches new tasks “using a fraction of the data” of single-task baselines, with performance scaling predictably with pretraining diversity. The data-efficiency claim is the entire commercial argument for pretraining corpora like ours. arXiv
