    TL;DR

    • Galbot’s Cang Yu stood up at Gasgoo’s July 3 embodied-AI conference and said the quiet part plainly: the brain is ready, the hands and the data are not, and capturing real-world data is “the most urgent problem to solve in the embodied AI space.” He put numbers on it. GPT ate three decades of internet data, Tesla FSD has 7 to 8 million drivers feeding it expert data, and embodied AI has neither a legacy corpus nor a factory willing to open its process data. That is the buyer-side statement of the entire capture thesis, from a founder who has already deployed. Gasgoo
    • Stanford’s Marcel Torné and Chelsea Finn released Freeform Preference Learning, an annotation method that drops the single overall reward and lets labellers define natural-language axes (speed, safety, placement quality, carefulness) and give pairwise preferences along each one. It is the clearest move yet toward multi-axis, promptable labeling of robot data, and it lands squarely in the validation layer, where a binary “which trajectory is better” has always thrown away most of the signal. arXiv 2606.32027, project site

Money is moving into the two layers Cang Yu named as the bottleneck, the data pipeline and the hands. SpeedBot via Gasgoo, Quanzhibo via Gasgoo

    Data collection

    • Cang Yu’s keynote is the capture signal of the day, and it is the most direct buyer-side confirmation of the thesis this column has logged in weeks. His framing: embodied AI’s data drought is far worse than autonomous driving’s, not marginally. Cars have kept the same structural blueprint since 1886 and self-driving is an upgrade to an established tool, so there is a fleet already generating expert data (he credits FSD’s 7 to 8 million drivers). Robots only became a physical form worth funding after large models showed they could replace labour, so there is no legacy data and, in his words, no factory willing to open up its proprietary process data. Even the best simulation still leaves a gap of several percentage points against the real world. Galbot’s own answer is telling: build demonstration production lines alongside partners in 3C, auto and auto-parts manufacturing and share the rights to their process data. That is a capture play built on access to real industrial environments, the exact axis where a validated-capture layer competes, and it is being run by the buyer rather than bought from a vendor. The gap he is describing is grounded environments plus the right to record in them, which is the whole argument. Gasgoo

    Data annotation & validation

    • Freeform Preference Learning is the fresh validation signal, and it is a real shift in how robot data gets graded, not a tooling tweak. Torné and Finn’s argument is that reward design is the bottleneck for policy improvement on long-horizon manipulation, that sparse success labels give too little signal, and that binary preferences collapse many competing notions of quality into one ambiguous number. Their fix: let annotators define preference axes in natural language, speed, safety, quality of placement, carefulness, and give pairwise preferences along each axis, then learn a multi-axis reward that returns a scalar when conditioned on a natural-language description of the axis, and train a promptable policy to optimise the combination. Read against the thesis, this is the annotation layer getting more expressive at exactly the point HuPa’s argument lives. A trajectory quality score generated and graded on one axis is the weak version of validation the TrainThemAI and China-buyer items keep exposing. Multi-axis, language-conditioned preference labeling is what a serious audit trail against a spec starts to look like, and it raises the bar for what “labeled” data has to carry. arXiv 2606.32027, project site

    Research papers worth a look

    • Freeform Preference Learning for Robotic Manipulation (Marcel Torné, Chelsea Finn and collaborators, Stanford, arXiv 2606.32027, July 2026). Learns robot rewards from freeform human preferences. Instead of asking which of two trajectories is better overall, annotators define natural-language preference axes (speed, safety, quality of placement, carefulness) and give pairwise preferences along each axis, and the method learns a multi-axis reward that returns a scalar when conditioned on a language description of the axis, then trains a promptable policy to optimise the chosen combination. The stated motivation is that reward design is the central bottleneck for autonomous policy improvement on long-horizon manipulation, sparse success labels give too little signal, and binary preferences collapse competing notions of quality into one ambiguous number. Worth reading as the annotation layer becoming multi-axis and language-conditioned, which is where a real audited-capture spec has to go. arXiv 2606.32027, project site
